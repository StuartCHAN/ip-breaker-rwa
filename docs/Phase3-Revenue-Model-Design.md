# Phase 3.0 Revenue Model Design

**Status:** Economic model baseline (pre-implementation)  
**Date:** 2026-07-21  
**Scope:** Revenue Token economics, eligibility, settlement, and distribution  
**Implementation:** No Solidity changes in Phase 3.0

## 1. Purpose and design boundary

Phase 2 established verified IP assets, verified participants, evidence review, license agreements, and escrow settlement. Phase 3 introduces a separate economic instrument only after those foundations exist.

This document defines the intended protocol economics. It does not determine the legal or regulatory classification of the instrument. Offering terms, jurisdiction, disclosures, tax, securities treatment, custody, sanctions procedures, and investor suitability require separate professional review before production deployment.

## 2. Decisions at a glance

| Question | Phase 3 baseline decision |
|---|---|
| What does the token represent? | A pro-rata contractual claim on a defined share of future distributable license revenue actually deposited into one asset's revenue vault. |
| Revenue scope | Single IP asset, across its eligible future license settlements. |
| Investor rights | Revenue rights only. No IP ownership, protocol governance, management control, principal guarantee, or holder-initiated redemption. |
| Transfer model | Compliance-restricted ERC-20-like transfer behavior. |
| Settlement asset | One immutable settlement asset per vault; production baseline is USDC. Native ETH may use a separate vault configuration. |
| Distribution | Pull payment using a cumulative revenue-per-share accumulator. No iteration over token holders. |
| Supply | Fixed/capped supply once revenue participation is activated. No discretionary dilution after activation. |
| Historical revenue | Excluded. Phase 2 revenue counters do not fund token claims. |

## 3. What the Revenue Token represents

### 3.1 Positive definition

One Revenue Token represents a proportional share of the **token-holder revenue pool** for one identified IP asset.

For token holder `i`:

```text
economic share_i = token balance_i / eligible total token supply
```

For each eligible revenue deposit:

```text
token-holder pool = realized license revenue × revenueShareBps / 10,000
```

The token holder may claim their accumulated portion of that pool, subject to the distribution and compliance rules below.

### 3.2 What it does not represent

The Revenue Token does **not** by itself represent:

- ownership of the underlying IP;
- ownership of the IP Asset NFT;
- a license to use the IP;
- ownership of a License Certificate NFT;
- voting or management control over the IP owner;
- governance rights over the protocol;
- debt principal or a guaranteed yield;
- a promise that license revenue will occur;
- a claim on all funds ever received by the licensor;
- a claim on revenue recorded before the tokenization cutover.

The IP Asset NFT, license agreements, and Revenue Token remain distinct instruments with distinct rights.

### 3.3 Claim boundary

A token claim exists only against funds that are:

1. generated by an eligible revenue source;
2. realized under the settlement rules;
3. allocated using the asset's immutable or governance-bounded `revenueShareBps`;
4. actually transferred into the matching distribution vault; and
5. accounted for by the vault's accumulator.

An event, database entry, expected invoice, or `totalRevenueByAsset` counter is not sufficient to create a funded claim.

## 4. Revenue source scope

### 4.1 Option comparison

| Scope | Benefits | Problems | Phase 3 decision |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single agreement | Simple legal boundary; easy attribution | Extremely fragmented liquidity and token supply; each new license requires a new token/vault | Not the default. May be useful for exceptional large agreements later. |
| Single asset | Revenue attribution follows one registered IP asset; multiple agreements can share one pool; understandable disclosures | Requires reliable asset-level routing and a cutover policy | **Selected baseline.** |
| Asset portfolio | Diversification; potentially smoother revenue | Requires portfolio governance, asset admission/removal rules, valuation, cross-asset accounting, and conflicts management | Deferred to a later phase. |

### 4.2 Selected model: one asset, one revenue program

The initial unit of tokenization is one `assetId`:

```text
IPAssetRegistry assetId
        │
        ├── Revenue Token
        └── Revenue Distribution Vault
```

Eligible revenue may come from multiple future license agreements for that asset, provided every payment is routed through the configured revenue program.

### 4.3 Initial eligible revenue

The safest initial scope is realized escrow-license revenue for the selected asset:

- normal `release()` to the licensor: eligible;
- `resolveDispute(true)` paying the licensor: eligible;
- `resolveDispute(false)` refunding the licensee: excluded;
- funded but unresolved escrow: excluded;
- Created, Funded, Active, or Disputed agreement balances: excluded;
- cancelled or refunded agreements: excluded;
- historical Phase 2 settlements: excluded;
- off-chain invoices or payments not deposited into the vault: excluded.

The direct offer path should remain excluded from the first implementation unless its payment flow is explicitly routed through the same revenue accounting boundary.

### 4.4 Cutover rule

Each revenue program has an activation timestamp or activation settlement index. Only funds routed after activation participate.

```text
Phase 2 historical revenue     Phase 3 eligible revenue
paid directly to licensor      routed into revenue program
           │                              │
           └── excluded          included after deposit
```

Existing `totalRevenueByAsset` values remain analytics. They must not be retroactively converted into token liabilities because the corresponding ETH has already been paid out.

### 4.5 Gross revenue and allocation

For the initial model, allocation should be deterministic and avoid discretionary expense deductions:

```text
gross realized revenue = amount successfully settled for licensor
token pool             = gross realized revenue × revenueShareBps / 10,000
licensor remainder     = gross realized revenue - token pool - explicit protocol fee
```

Any protocol fee must be disclosed, bounded, and applied before the token pool deposit using a documented formula. Arbitrary off-chain “net income” deductions are out of scope for the first version because they make on-chain claims dependent on unauditable accounting judgments.

## 5. Investor rights

### 5.1 Revenue right

Token holders have the right to claim accrued, funded distributions calculated by the vault. Accrual is proportional to eligible token balances during each deposit interval.

Transfers do not give a buyer the seller's already accrued but unclaimed revenue. The accounting model checkpoints both parties so:

- the seller retains distributions accrued before transfer;
- the buyer participates only from the transferred balance after transfer;
- no distribution is counted twice.

### 5.2 Governance rights

The baseline token has no holder governance rights. Token holders cannot, merely by holding Revenue Tokens:

- change license terms or pricing;
- sell or transfer the underlying IP;
- replace the asset owner, verifier, or arbiter;
- change `revenueShareBps`;
- mint additional Revenue Tokens;
- change the settlement asset;
- upgrade protocol contracts;
- redirect escrow funds.

Protocol emergency administration, if later implemented, is a system control and not token-holder governance.

### 5.3 Redemption rights

There is no holder-initiated redemption for principal, IP ownership, or a fixed amount of settlement assets.

The supported exit mechanisms are:

- claim accrued distributions; and
- transfer the token to another eligible participant, if a compliant market exists.

A future issuer buyback or wind-down program would be a separate, explicitly funded mechanism. It must not be implied by the initial token.

### 5.4 No yield guarantee

If the IP asset produces no eligible license revenue, token distributions are zero. Token supply does not create revenue by itself.

## 6. Transfer rules

### 6.1 Option comparison

| Model | Benefits | Risks | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|
| Permissionless ERC-20 | Maximum composability and liquidity | Cannot enforce offering eligibility, sanctions, jurisdiction, or investor restrictions | Rejected for the baseline. |
| Compliance-restricted transfer | Eligibility enforced consistently; better fit for RWA distribution | Reduced composability; needs recovery and liveness policies | **Selected baseline.** |

### 6.2 Baseline compliance policy

For ordinary transfers:

```text
sender is permitted to transfer
AND receiver has active ROLE_INVESTOR
AND receiver satisfies offering-specific eligibility policy
AND transfer is not paused or blocked by the offering policy
```

`ROLE_INVESTOR` alone is intentionally broad in Phase 2 and is not sufficient for every production offering. Phase 3 needs an offering-specific eligibility policy for jurisdiction, accreditation or investor category, sanctions, concentration limits, and applicable transfer restrictions.

Minting is allowed only to eligible recipients. Transfers to the zero address follow burn rules, not receiver eligibility rules.

### 6.3 Invalid identity after acquisition

Suspension, expiration, or revocation does not silently confiscate tokens or erase accrued revenue.

Baseline behavior:

- new purchases and ordinary outgoing transfers may be blocked by policy;
- new claims are paused until the holder is eligible again or a lawful recovery process is used;
- accrued balances remain accounted for;
- an authorized recovery/forced-transfer path may move holdings to an eligible replacement address with auditable events.

A permanently revoked holder creates a liveness requirement. The implementation design must define a narrowly authorized recovery process before production deployment.

### 6.4 Transfer hooks and distribution accounting

Every mint, transfer, burn, recovery, or forced transfer must checkpoint distribution accounting before balances change. Compliance checks and accounting hooks must be composed so a failed compliance check cannot partially update rewards.

## 7. Settlement asset

### 7.1 One settlement asset per vault

Each asset revenue vault uses one immutable settlement asset. A single accumulator must not mix ETH, USDC, and unrelated ERC-20 values because their units and economic values are not interchangeable.

```text
assetId 42 / USDC vault  -> USDC accumulator
assetId 42 / ETH vault   -> separate ETH accumulator, if enabled
```

### 7.2 ETH

Benefits:

- directly compatible with the current LicenseEscrow payment path;
- no token approval or nonstandard ERC-20 behavior;
- simple native-value settlement.

Limitations:

- volatile denomination for real-world licensing;
- native ETH transfers require careful recipient and reentrancy handling;
- harder to reconcile with fiat-denominated agreements.

ETH may be supported through a dedicated native-asset vault, but it should not share accounting with an ERC-20 vault.

### 7.3 USDC

USDC is the preferred production baseline because it provides a stable settlement unit and clearer agreement/accounting denomination.

The design must pin the exact token contract per chain and must not assume that every asset named “USDC” is equivalent. Decimal handling, blacklist behavior, upgradeability, custody, and chain-specific issuer risk must be disclosed.

### 7.4 Other ERC-20 tokens

Other settlement tokens are deferred until explicitly allowlisted. Initial vaults should reject:

- fee-on-transfer tokens;
- rebasing tokens;
- tokens with unreliable return values unless safely wrapped;
- tokens whose balance can change without a transfer;
- unreviewed bridge-wrapped assets.

Deposits should account using actual balance deltas where appropriate. No implicit swap, oracle conversion, or cross-token netting occurs inside a distribution vault.

### 7.5 Migration from current ETH settlement

The current LicenseEscrow pays ETH directly to the licensor and only increments an analytics counter. Phase 3 implementation must introduce an explicit routing boundary, for example:

```text
eligible license settlement
        ↓
revenue router
        ├── licensor remainder
        ├── protocol fee (if any)
        └── token-holder pool → distribution vault
```

If USDC is selected, license settlement must occur in USDC or use an explicit, separately reviewed conversion mechanism. The accounting layer must never pretend that already-paid ETH is still available as USDC-backed revenue.

## 8. Distribution model

### 8.1 Selected approach

Use both:

- **pull payment:** holders call `claim()` to withdraw accrued revenue; and
- **accumulator per share:** every deposit increases a global cumulative revenue index.

Do not push funds to all holders during deposit. An on-chain holder loop is unbounded, expensive, and vulnerable to a single failing recipient.

### 8.2 Core accounting

Let:

```text
P = fixed precision scalar
A = cumulative revenue per token share
D = new distributable deposit
S = eligible total token supply
```

On deposit, when `S > 0`:

```text
A_new = A_old + floor(D × P / S)
```

For each account, maintain enough checkpoint state to calculate:

```text
claimable(account)
  = storedPending(account)
  + balance(account) × A / P
  - rewardDebt(account)
```

Before any balance change:

1. settle the sender's pending revenue;
2. settle the receiver's pending revenue;
3. change token balances;
4. update both reward-debt checkpoints to the new balances.

This ensures transferred tokens carry only future revenue participation, not the seller's historical accrual.

### 8.3 Claim flow

```text
holder calls claim()
        ↓
validate claim eligibility
        ↓
checkpoint holder
        ↓
set claimable amount to zero / mark withdrawn
        ↓
transfer settlement asset
        ↓
emit claim event
```

Claims must use checks-effects-interactions and reentrancy protection. A failed transfer must revert the entire claim so accounting and vault balances remain consistent.

### 8.4 Deposit rules

- Deposits must identify the revenue program and settlement asset.
- Only authorized protocol settlement paths may classify deposits as license revenue.
- Unsolicited token transfers must not silently increase the accounted accumulator.
- `totalAccountedRevenue` must increase by the amount accepted for distribution.
- If supply is zero, the deposit must revert or follow a predefined reserve policy; it must not divide by zero or allocate revenue ambiguously.
- Rounding dust must remain tracked and be rolled into later distributions or handled by a disclosed terminal policy.

### 8.5 Solvency invariant

At all times:

```text
vault settlement balance
  >= total accounted deposits
     - total successful claims
```

For a non-rebasing, non-fee settlement asset, expected accounting is:

```text
totalAccountedRevenue
  = totalClaimed
  + totalOutstandingClaimable
  + trackedRoundingRemainder
```

The implementation phase must add invariant tests for solvency, conservation of distributions, transfer checkpoint correctness, and absence of double claims.

## 9. Token supply and dilution

The Revenue Token supply is fixed or capped when the revenue program activates. After activation:

- arbitrary minting is prohibited;
- token splits or recapitalization require a new disclosed program or migration;
- burns do not retroactively reallocate already accrued revenue;
- new supply cannot claim distributions deposited before its checkpoint.

This prevents the asset owner or protocol administrator from diluting existing revenue rights after funds begin accruing.

## 10. Revenue program lifecycle

```text
Draft
  │ configure asset, settlement token, share, supply, disclosures
  ▼
Configured
  │ mint only to eligible investors
  ▼
Active
  │ accept eligible revenue and permit compliant transfers/claims
  ▼
Wind-down
  │ stop new revenue enrollment; allow final claims
  ▼
Closed
```

Activation must be explicit. An IP Asset NFT transfer must not silently change the Revenue Token's economic terms or redirect its vault.

## 11. Required implementation modules for Phase 3.1

This design implies separate responsibilities:

| Module | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| Revenue program/factory | Bind `assetId`, token, vault, settlement asset, revenue share, activation, and disclosures. |
| Revenue Token | Balances, capped supply, compliance-aware transfers, distribution checkpoints. |
| Distribution Vault | Receive accounted revenue, update accumulator, expose pull claims, preserve solvency. |
| Revenue Router | Split eligible realized license revenue between licensor, protocol fee, and token-holder vault. |
| Offering eligibility policy | Enforce investor and jurisdiction-specific transfer/claim rules beyond the broad Phase 2 role. |

These responsibilities may be combined only if security review shows that the resulting contract remains understandable and testable.

## 12. Non-goals

Phase 3 baseline does not include:

- portfolio tokens;
- token-holder governance;
- guaranteed redemption or yield;
- lending against Revenue Tokens;
- automated market making;
- oracle-based cross-currency conversion;
- off-chain revenue attestation as a substitute for funded deposits;
- automatic tokenization of every registered asset;
- retroactive distribution of Phase 2 revenue.

## 13. Open decisions before Solidity implementation

The following must be resolved and documented before Phase 3.1 coding:

1. exact legal issuer and contractual obligor for the revenue right;
2. exact chain and USDC contract, or whether the first technical prototype remains ETH-denominated;
3. fixed token supply and decimals;
4. `revenueShareBps`, protocol fee, and whether either can ever change;
5. who may activate or wind down a revenue program;
6. offering-specific investor eligibility and jurisdiction rules;
7. recovery process for lost keys, suspended investors, and permanently revoked investors;
8. pause powers and treatment of already accrued claims during a pause;
9. behavior when the IP Asset NFT changes owner;
10. treatment of settlement refunds, chargebacks, or legal reversals after distribution;
11. dust policy and terminal vault closure;
12. upgrade and migration strategy.

No Revenue Token should be deployed until these parameters are represented in immutable configuration, bounded governance, or signed offering documentation.
